# Chapter 3: The On-Time System (Overview)

## The Core Framework

After 100+ events and 20+ years, here's what I've learned:

**Tournaments don't fail because of bad luck. They fail because of bad systems.**

The solution? A simple, repeatable framework that guarantees your event starts on time — every single time.

I call it **The On-Time System**. It has 5 steps:

1. **Work Backward** from start time
2. **Plan for the Problem** (not the ideal)
3. **Staff Adequately** (more than you think you need)
4. **Communicate Constantly** (before they ask)
5. **Measure Everything** (what gets tracked gets managed)

That's it. Five steps. Every successful tournament uses these — whether they know it or not.

---

## Step 1: Work Backward from Start Time

This is THE most important concept in tournament operations.

### Most People Do This:
- "Let's set up at 8AM"
- "We'll get ready and start around 10"
- "We'll see how it goes"

### The Right Way:
- "We start at 10:00 AM"
- "First match called at 9:45 AM"
- "Weigh-ins close at 9:15 AM"
- "Weigh-ins open at 8:30 AM"
- "Doors open at 8:00 AM"
- "Staff arrives at 7:30 AM"
- "Venue setup complete at 7:00 AM"

**You work BACKWARD from your start time to determine everything else.**

This is backward planning. It sounds simple — and it is. But almost no one does it.

### The Backward Planning Formula

| Time | Task |
|------|------|
| **Start Time** | First match on ALL mats |
| T - 15 min | Final announcements, mats cleared |
| T - 30 min | First call for division |
| T - 45 min | Weigh-ins close for first division |
| T - 60 min | Weigh-ins complete |
| T - 90 min | Doors open, weigh-ins begin |
| T - 120 min | Staff arrives, final prep |
| T - 150 min | Venue setup complete |

**Rule:** Never compress the timeline. If you need more time, move the start time — don't rush.

## Step 2: Plan for the Problem (Not the Ideal)

Ideal days don't happen. Here's what will actually go wrong:

- A wrestler shows up late to weigh-ins
- A bracket has an error you didn't catch
- A ref doesn't show up
- A computer crashes
- A parent complains about seeding
- A wrestler misses their match

**Every tournament has problems. The question is: Are you ready to solve them?**

### The Preparation Framework

For every potential problem, have a solution ready:

| Potential Problem | Your Solution |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Ref no-show | Backup ref on call, or you ref |
| Computer crash | Backup laptop, paper brackets |
| Bracket error | Time built in for corrections |
| Weigh-in dispute | Certified scale for re-weigh |
| Parent complaint | Floor manager handles |
| Medical emergency | EMT on-site or nearby |

## Step 3: Staff Adequately

The #1 cause of tournament problems? **Understaffing.**

### Minimum Staffing Requirements

| Role | Per Mat | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Referees | 1.0 | 1 per mat, always |
| Table workers | 2.0 | Scorer + runner |
| Floor manager | 1 per 6 mats | Overall coordination |
| Weigh-in staff | 2 total | Not per mat |
| Registration | 2 total | Handle check-in |

**Pro tip:** Always have 1-2 extra people on standby. Not "optional" — "on standby."

### The Overstaffing Secret

Here's a secret most TD don't realize:

**It's better to have too many staff than too few.**

Why?
- Extra people can be reassigned to emergencies
- Staff can take breaks (no one burns out)
- You look professional, not disorganized
- Things get done faster

The cost of one extra table worker? Maybe $100.
The cost of running late because you're understaffed? Thousands in lost future revenue.

## Step 4: Communicate Constantly

Parents don't ask questions when they already know the answer.

### The Communication Rule

**Tell them what they need to know BEFORE they need to know it.**

### Communication Timeline

| When | What to Communicate |
|------|---------------------|
| 4 weeks out | Registration open, date, venue |
| 2 weeks out | Schedule, weigh-in options |
| 1 week out | Final details, start times |
| 3 days out | Reminder, any changes |
| 1 day out | Final confirmations |
| Morning of | Where to park, check-in location |
| During event | PA announcements, bracket updates |
| After event | Thank you, next event date |

### What Must Be Communicated

Every family needs to know:
- [ ] Exact start time (and that you'll start ON TIME)
- [ ] Weigh-in windows
- [ ] Session times (if multi-session)
- [ ] Where to park
- [ ] Entry/pickup procedures
- [ ] What to bring
- [ ] Emergency contacts

If they're wondering about it — tell them before they ask.

## Step 5: Measure Everything

What gets measured gets managed.

### The Metrics to Track

After EVERY event, record these:

1. **Start Time Actual vs. Planned** — Did you start on time?
2. **End Time** — How long did the event run?
3. **Mat Efficiency** — Were mats active or empty?
4. **Registration Count** — How many participated?
5. **Return Rate** — What % are repeat families?
6. **Staff Count** — How many people worked?
7. **Issues Log** — What went wrong?

### The Post-Event Review

Within 48 hours of every event, review:
- What went right?
- What went wrong?
- What would we change?
- What did we learn?

Write it down. Update your playbook. Get better every event.

## The Trust Metric

Here's a concept I use at every Valhalla event:

**Empty mat = management failure.**

If a mat is empty while wrestlers are waiting to compete, something went wrong. Not the ref, not the bracket — the management.

### How to Keep Mats Active

- Next wrestler ready at staging area
- Runner knows exactly where next match is
- Referee ready immediately after previous match
- No gaps between matches (more than 2 minutes is too long)

This is the measure of a professional tournament. Not fancy awards, not perfect brackets — every mat active, always.

Every tournament should make these 5 promises — and the On-Time System delivers on all of them:
