# Chapter 2: The Numbers — Why Systems Matter

## Industry Benchmarks: The Math No One Talks About

Here's what tournament directors never calculate — but absolutely should:

**How many matches can one mat handle per hour?**

This single number determines everything:
- How many mats you need
- How many refs you need
- How many table workers you need
- How long your event will run
- Whether parents are happy or frustrated

After analyzing hundreds of events (including Tim's own Valhalla data), here's what the numbers show:

### Average Bouts Per Mat Per Hour

| Event Type | Bouts/Mat/Hour | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| Youth Tournament (experienced) | 6-8 | Fast-paced, quick matches |
| Youth Tournament (mixed skill) | 4-6 | Average competition |
| Middle School | 5-7 | Varies by skill level |
| High School | 4-6 | More competitive, longer matches |
| Double Elimination (finals) | 3-4 | Championship rounds take longer |

**The Valhalla Standard: 6 bouts per mat per hour** — This is our target. It means:
- A 3-hour session = ~18 matches per mat
- 100 wrestlers ÷ 18 matches = need ~6 mats
- With 6 mats running = smooth, fast event

---

## The Capacity Formula

Here's the formula every tournament director should know:

```
Total mats × Bouts per hour × Hours in session = Total matches possible

Example:
6 mats × 6 bouts/hour × 3 hours = 108 matches per session
```

**What does this tell you?**

If you have 150 wrestlers in a division and each needs 3 matches:
- You need 150 × 3 = 450 match slots
- At 108 matches per session, you need ~4.2 sessions
- That's impossible in one day

**Solution:** Multi-session events or multiple days.

## The Cost of Starting Late

Let's quantify the impact of starting late:

### Direct Costs
- Staff sitting idle (you're still paying them)
- Venue running overtime
- Venue staff waiting
- Referee schedule disrupted
- Food vendors affected

### Indirect Costs (The Big Hit)
- 20% of families don't return = lost lifetime value of ~$3,300/year
- Negative word of mouth = fewer new registrations
- Reputation damage = long-term attendance decline

**Real Example:**
- Tournament A runs late (starts 45 min late, ends 8PM)
- Tournament B runs on time (starts 9AM, ends noon)
- Both charge $55, both have 300 wrestlers
- Tournament B's families had a BETTER experience, will come back
- Tournament A's families are frustrated, will look for alternatives

**5-year impact:** If Tournament A loses just 10 families per year to better-run events, that's $16,500 in lost revenue.

## The Break-Even Analysis

Here's the truth about tournament economics:

### Revenue Sources
- Registration fees
- Spectator admission
- Concessions (sometimes)
- Sponsorships
- Vendor fees

### Fixed Costs (Don't Change with Attendance)
- Venue rental
- Insurance
- TrackWrestling subscription
- Base awards order
- Marketing (if any)

### Variable Costs (Scale with Attendance)
- Referee payments
- Table worker payments
- Extra mats rental
- Awards overage
- Concession supplies
- Staff food

### The Break-Even Point

Let's say your tournament costs $6,000 to run:

| Entry Fee | Families Needed | Breakeven |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| $40 | 150 | Minimum viable |
| $50 | 120 | Comfortable |
| $55 | 109 | Good margin |
| $65 | 92 | Strong profit |

**Target:** Always know your break-even number. If registrations drop below it, you need to either raise prices, cut costs, or cancel.

## The Staffing Math

How many people do you need? Here's the formula:

### Referees
- 1 ref per mat minimum
- Add 1 backup ref per 4 mats
- More refs = faster bracket progression

### Table Workers
- 1 scorer + 1 runner per mat
- Minimum: 2 per mat
- Ideal: 3 per mat (rotation breaks)

### Floor Manager
- 1 per 8 mats
- More complex events need more oversight

### Support Staff
- Weigh-in: 2-4 people
- Registration: 2 people
- Awards: 2 people
- Runner: 1-2 people

**Example: 6-mat tournament**
- Referees: 6 + 2 backup = 8
- Table workers: 6 × 2 = 12
- Floor manager: 1
- Support staff: 8
- **Total staff needed: ~29 people**

## The Mat Efficiency Index

Here's a metric I developed for Valhalla:

**Mat Efficiency Index (MEI)** = Actual bouts run / Expected bouts

| MEI Score | Rating | What It Means |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| 0.9 - 1.1 | ✅ Excellent | Running at expected capacity |
| 0.7 - 0.9 | ⚠️ Warning | Some mat downtime |
| Below 0.7 | ❌ Poor | Significant problems |

**How to measure:**
- Track actual start/end times for each round
- Compare to planned schedule
- Calculate daily MEI
- If below 0.8 → review what went wrong

## Data-Driven Decision Making

Here's what you should track after every event:

### Key Metrics
1. **Start time accuracy** — Did you start on time?
2. **End time** — Did you finish when expected?
3. **Mat efficiency** — How close to capacity?
4. **Matches per wrestler** — Did everyone get enough?
5. **Return rate** — What % came back?
6. **Staff cost per wrestler** — Efficiency indicator

### Year-Over-Year Tracking
- Compare these metrics annually
- Identify trends (improving or declining)
- Adjust systems based on data
