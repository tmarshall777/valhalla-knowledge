# Chapter 1: The Tournament Director's Mindset

## The Shift from Event Organizer to Service Provider

Here's a truth most tournament directors never realize:

**You're not running an event. You're running a service business.**

The moment you understand this, everything changes.

Before, you thought of yourself as someone who organizes wrestling matches. You book a venue, set up mats, get refs, run brackets, hand out medals. That's an event.

But here's what event organizers miss: Parents don't wake up excited about brackets. They don't drive 90 minutes thinking about double elimination formats. They don't drive home discussing bracketology.

They think about their kid. Did they have a good experience? Did they wrestle? Did they get better? Did it start on time? Did we get home at a reasonable hour?

**That's** what they're evaluating. That's the product they're "buying."

Your product isn't a wrestling tournament. Your product is an experience for their child. And experiences are judged by how people *feel* — not by how professionally you managed the bracket.

---

## Understanding the Customer

Every customer at your tournament fits into one of three categories:

### Category 1: Parents
They care about:
- **Time**: "Will this take all day?"
- **Value**: "Was it worth $50?"
- **Convenience**: "Where do I go? When does my kid wrestle?"
- **Their kid's experience**: "Did my kid have fun? Did they get matches?"

They will NOT remember:
- How perfect your brackets were
- How smoothly you handled a disputed call
- The clever way you organized weigh-ins

They WILL remember:
- Start time (did it start on time?)
- End time (did we get home reasonable?)
- Their kid's result (did they win?)
- Overall feeling (was this worth it?)

### Category 2: Coaches
They care about:
- **Competition level**: "Are there quality opponents for my kids?"
- **Seeding accuracy**: "Will my state champion be seeded correctly?"
- **Logistics**: "Will my kids actually get matches?"
- **Communication**: "Do I know when and where my kids are wrestling?"

They will NOT remember:
- Your venue decoration
- Your custom awards (unless they're terrible)

They WILL remember:
- Did my kids get enough matches?
- Was the re-seeding handled correctly?
- Would I bring my team back?

### Category 3: Athletes
They care about:
- **Matches**: "Did I actually wrestle?"
- **Competition**: "Were the other kids at my level?"
- **Awards**: "Do I get a cool medal?"
- **Fun**: "Was this fun or boring?"

They will remember:
- Did I win?
- Did I get a cool medal?
- Was it fun?

## The Economics of Tournaments

Let's talk money, because if you don't understand the economics, you won't invest in quality.

Let's say you run a tournament with:
- 300 wrestlers
- $55 entry fee
- 2 mats
- 8-hour event

**Revenue**: 300 × $55 = $16,500

**Costs**:
- Venue: $2,000
- Referees (16 hours @ $25/hr): $1,600
- Table workers: $800
- Awards: $1,500
- TrackWrestling: $200
- Misc (food, supplies): $500

**Total Costs**: ~$6,600  
**Net Profit**: ~$9,900

That's a healthy profit. But here's the catch:

### The Real Cost of Bad Service

What if your tournament runs poorly?

Let's say because of late starts and bad management:
- 20% of families don't come back = 60 lost customers
- Each family brings 2-3 kids = potential 150+ lost entries next year
- Word of mouth spreads = fewer new customers

**Lost revenue**: 60 × $55 = $3,300 *in repeat business alone*

But it gets worse. Let's say you lose the reputation, and over 3 years:
- First year: 300 wrestlers
- Second year: 260 (40 fewer due to reputation)
- Third year: 220 (another drop)

**Three-year revenue loss**: 80 registrations × 3 years × $55 = **$13,200 lost**

The $3,000 you "saved" by cutting corners? You just lost $13,200 in future business.

**Quality drives profitability.** Not cut corners. Not minimum viable events.

## Industry Benchmarks: The Math No One Talks About

Here's what tournament directors never calculate — but absolutely should:

**How many matches can one mat handle per hour?**

This single number determines everything:
- How many mats you need
- How many refs you need
- How many table workers you need
- How long your event will run
- Whether parents are happy or frustrated

After analyzing hundreds of events (including Tim's own Valhalla data), here's what the numbers show:

### Average Bouts Per Mat Per Hour

| Event Type | Bouts/Mat/Hour | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| Youth Tournament (experienced) | 6-8 | Fast-paced, quick matches |
| Youth Tournament (mixed skill) | 4-6 | Average competition |
| Middle School | 5-7 | Varies by skill level |
| High School | 4-6 | More competitive, longer matches |
| Double Elimination (finals) | 3-4 | Championship rounds take longer |

**The Valhalla Standard: 6 bouts per mat per hour** — This is our target. It means:
- A 3-hour session = ~18 matches per mat
- 100 wrestlers ÷ 18 matches = need ~6 mats
- With 6 mats running = smooth, fast event

---

## The Capacity Formula

Here's the formula every tournament director should know:

```
Total mats × Bouts per hour × Hours in session = Total matches possible

Example:
6 mats × 6 bouts/hour × 3 hours = 108 matches per session
```

**What does this tell you?**

If you have 150 wrestlers in a division and each needs 3 matches:
- You need 150 × 3 = 450 match slots
- At 108 matches per session, you need ~4.2 sessions
- That's impossible in one day

**Solution:** Multi-session events or multiple days.

## The Break-Even Analysis

Here's the truth about tournament economics:

### Revenue Sources
- Registration fees
- Spectator admission
- Concessions (sometimes)
- Sponsorships
- Vendor fees

### Fixed Costs (Don't Change with Attendance)
- Venue rental
- Insurance
- TrackWrestling subscription
- Base awards order
- Marketing (if any)

### Variable Costs (Scale with Attendance)
- Referee payments
- Table worker payments
- Extra mats rental
- Awards overage
- Concession supplies
- Staff food

### The Break-Even Point

Let's say your tournament costs $6,000 to run:

| Entry Fee | Families Needed | Breakeven |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| $40 | 150 | Minimum viable |
| $50 | 120 | Comfortable |
| $55 | 109 | Good margin |
| $65 | 92 | Strong profit |

**Target:** Always know your break-even number. If registrations drop below it, you need to either raise prices, cut costs, or cancel.

## The Staffing Math

How many people do you need? Here's the formula:

### Referees
- 1 ref per mat minimum
- Add 1 backup ref per 4 mats
- More refs = faster bracket progression

### Table Workers
- 1 scorer + 1 runner per mat
- Minimum: 2 per mat
- Ideal: 3 per mat (rotation breaks)

### Floor Manager
- 1 per 8 mats
- More complex events need more oversight

### Support Staff
- Weigh-in: 2-4 people
- Registration: 2 people
- Awards: 2 people
- Runner: 1-2 people

**Example: 6-mat tournament**
- Referees: 6 + 2 backup = 8
- Table workers: 6 × 2 = 12
- Floor manager: 1
- Support staff: 8
- **Total staff needed: ~29 people**

Here's a metric I developed for Valhalla:

**Mat Efficiency Index (MEI)** = Actual bouts run / Expected bouts

| MEI Score | Rating | What It Means |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| 0.9 - 1.1 | ✅ Excellent | Running at expected capacity |

**How to measure:**

- Compare to planned schedule
- Calculate daily MEI
- If below 0.8 → review what went wrong

## The Core Framework

After 100+ events and 20+ years, here's what I've learned:

**Tournaments don't fail because of bad luck. They fail because of bad systems.**

The solution? A simple, repeatable framework that guarantees your event starts on time — every single time.

I call it **The On-Time System**. It has 5 steps:

1. **Work Backward** from start time
2. **Plan for the Problem** (not the ideal)
3. **Staff Adequately** (more than you think you need)
4. **Communicate Constantly** (before they ask)
5. **Measure Everything** (what gets tracked gets managed)

That's it. Five steps. Every successful tournament uses these — whether they know it or not.

## Step 1: Work Backward from Start Time

This is THE most important concept in tournament operations.

### Most People Do This:
- "Let's set up at 8AM"
- "We'll get ready and start around 10"
- "We'll see how it goes"

### The Right Way:
- "We start at 10:00 AM"
- "First match called at 9:45 AM"
- "Weigh-ins close at 9:15 AM"
- "Weigh-ins open at 8:30 AM"
- "Doors open at 8:00 AM"
- "Staff arrives at 7:30 AM"
- "Venue setup complete at 7:00 AM"

**You work BACKWARD from your start time to determine everything else.**

This is backward planning. It sounds simple — and it is. But almost no one does it.

### The Backward Planning Formula

| Time | Task |
|------|------|
| **Start Time** | First match on ALL mats |
| T - 15 min | Final announcements, mats cleared |
| T - 30 min | First call for division |
| T - 45 min | Weigh-ins close for first division |
| T - 60 min | Weigh-ins complete |
| T - 90 min | Doors open, weigh-ins begin |
| T - 120 min | Staff arrives, final prep |
| T - 150 min | Venue setup complete |

**Rule:** Never compress the timeline. If you need more time, move the start time — don't rush.

## Step 2: Plan for the Problem (Not the Ideal)

Ideal days don't happen. Here's what will actually go wrong:

- A wrestler shows up late to weigh-ins

- A ref doesn't show up
- A computer crashes
- A parent complains about seeding
- A wrestler misses their match

**Every tournament has problems. The question is: Are you ready to solve them?**

### The Preparation Framework

For every potential problem, have a solution ready:

| Potential Problem | Your Solution |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Ref no-show | Backup ref on call, or you ref |
| Computer crash | Backup laptop, paper brackets |
| Bracket error | Time built in for corrections |
| Weigh-in dispute | Certified scale for re-weigh |
| Parent complaint | Floor manager handles |
| Medical emergency | EMT on-site or nearby |

## Step 3: Staff Adequately

The #1 cause of tournament problems? **Understaffing.**

### Minimum Staffing Requirements

| Role | Per Mat | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Referees | 1.0 | 1 per mat, always |
| Table workers | 2.0 | Scorer + runner |
| Floor manager | 1 per 6 mats | Overall coordination |
| Weigh-in staff | 2 total | Not per mat |
| Registration | 2 total | Handle check-in |

**Pro tip:** Always have 1-2 extra people on standby. Not "optional" — "on standby."

### The Overstaffing Secret

Here's a secret most TD don't realize:

**It's better to have too many staff than too few.**

Why?
- Extra people can be reassigned to emergencies
- Staff can take breaks (no one burns out)
- You look professional, not disorganized
- Things get done faster

The cost of one extra table worker? Maybe $100.
The cost of running late because you're understaffed? Thousands in lost future revenue.

## Session Planning

How many sessions per day? Here's how to decide:

### Factors to Consider

| Factor | Single Session | Multi-Session |
|--------|----------------|---------------|

| **Mats available** | 8+ | Fewer than 6 |
| **Time of day** | Single is easier | Split for timing |
| **Divisions** | Fewer divisions | Many divisions |
| **Venue constraints** | 1 session day | Multiple days |

### The Multi-Session Framework

| Session | Divisions | Start Time | Approximate Duration |
|---------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Morning** | 6U, 10U | 9:00 AM | 3-4 hours |
| **Noon** | 8U, 12U | 12:00 PM | 3-4 hours |
| **Evening** | 15U, HS | 3:00 PM | 3-4 hours |

**Note:** This is Tim's Valhalla format. Adjust based on your event size.

## Weigh-In Windows

How long should weigh-ins be? Here's the formula:

### Standard Formula

```
(# of wrestlers) ÷ (mats × bouts/hour) = minutes needed for weigh-in
```

### Example

- 100 wrestlers in session
- 4 mats running
- 6 bouts/mat/hour = 24 matches/hour
- Need ~4-5 hours of match time
- Add 1 hour for weigh-in = 5-6 hour session

### Typical Windows

| Division Size | Weigh-In Window |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Small (under 50) | 30 minutes |
| Medium (50-100) | 45-60 minutes |
| Large (100+) | 60-90 minutes |

## When Things Go Wrong (Because They Will)

Even the best-planned events have problems. The difference between amateur and professional tournaments is how problems are handled.

This chapter gives you protocols for the most common issues.

---

## Problem Categories

### 1. Weight Disputes
**Scenario:** Parent claims wrestler is at correct weight but scale shows otherwise.

**Solution:**
- Have certified scale for verification
- Allow one re-weigh in singlet (no clothes)
- If dispute persists, floor manager decides
- Document all disputes

### 2. Bracket Errors
**Scenario:** Wrestler in wrong bracket, seeding wrong, or missing from bracket.

**Solution:**
- Floor manager reviews
- If before match: fix and re-print
- If after match: match stands, fix for future rounds
- Document all errors

### 3. No-Shows
**Scenario:** Wrestler doesn't report to mat when called.

**Solution:**
- Call name 3 times
- Wait 2 minutes
- Default the match
- Document for bracket purposes

### 4. Referee Issues
**Scenario:** Referee calls in sick, or makes controversial call.

**Solution:**
- Have backup ref on call
- Floor manager discusses with ref
- If call wrong, may discuss (but officials' calls final)
- Never argue with refs publicly

### 5. Technical Failures
**Scenario:** Computer crashes, WiFi fails, TrackWrestling freezes.

**Solution:**
- Switch to paper brackets
- Continue matches manually
- Have backup laptop ready
- Never stop event for tech issues

### 6. Parent/Coach Complaints
**Scenario:** Someone complains about seeding, match outcome, or scheduling.

**Solution:**
- Listen calmly
- Don't argue
- Floor manager handles
- If escalated, TD meets privately
- Never argue in public

---

## The Escalation Framework

| Level | Who Handles | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **1** | Table Worker | Minor score corrections |
| **2** | Floor Manager | Bracket errors, disputes |
| **3** | TD | Major issues, refunds, venue |

## Emergency Protocols

### Medical Emergency
- Stop the match
- Clear the mat
- Call for EMT/Trainer
- If serious, call 911
- Document everything

### Facility Emergency
- Fire: Evacuate per venue plan
- Power outage: Continue with manual scoring
- HVAC failure: Assess safety, decide continue/cancel

### Security Issue
- Contact venue security
- If serious, call 911
- Document everything

- Listen first
- Solve problems privately
- Document issues
- Learn from mistakes

### Don't
- Don't argue publicly
- Don't make exceptions for anyone
- Don't panic
- Don't ignore problems
- Don't blame staff publicly

## What Is a Dual Tournament?

A dual tournament is team-vs-team competition where two teams face off in a scheduled match format. Unlike bracket tournaments (single/double elimination), duals are scored on match wins.

### Format Overview

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Teams** | Typically 8-16 teams |
| **Format** | Each team fields wrestlers at each weight class |
| **Scoring** | Most match wins wins the dual |
| **Duration** | 3-5 hours depending on team size |

---

## Key Differences from Bracket Tournaments

### Scoring Structure

| Bracket Tournament | Dual Tournament |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Individual placement | Team score |
| Medal awards | Team placement |
| Single elimination possible | Full lineup required |
| Seeding important | Matchups matter |

### Match Management

- Every match counts for team score
- Team lineup must be submitted
- Weight classes matter more
- Cannot scratch easily (affects team)

---

## Setting Up a Dual Tournament

### Pre-Event Requirements

1. **Team Registration**
   - Collect team registrations
   - Require lineup in advance
   - Know weight classes each team will fill

2. **Venue Setup**
   - Mats in central area
   - Team seating areas
   - Scoring table with PA
   - Scoreboard visible to all

3. **Schedule**
   - Round-robin or bracket format
   - Typically 1 day for duals
   - Set start times (9 AM standard)

---

## Dual Tournament Scoring

### Standard Scoring

| Result | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Win by pin | 6 points |
| Win by tech fall (15+) | 5 points |
| Major decision (8-14) | 4 points |
| Decision (1-7) | 3 points |
| Loss | 0 points |

### Dual Winner

Team with most points after all matches wins the dual.

Alternative: Best-of series (wrestle 3, win 2)

---

## Team Management

### Pre-Dual Communication

- Confirm teams attending
- Collect lineup from each coach
- Verify weights
- Set schedule for weigh-ins

### Day-of Procedures

- Team check-in
- Weigh-ins (may differ from bracket events)
- Team lineup submission
- Bracket/bracket generation
- Scoring updates after each round

---

## Special Considerations for Duals

### Venue Space

Duals need less space than big bracket tournaments, but more team areas:
- Bleacher space for teams
- Warm-up area for each team
- Coaching area

### Timing

Duals typically run faster:
- Quick matches
- No long breaks between rounds
- Set schedule per round

### Awards

- Team awards (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
- Individual awards may vary
- Often simpler than bracket events

## Why Weigh-Ins Matter

Weigh-ins are often where tournaments lose control. Get this wrong, and your entire day is chaotic.

This chapter covers three weigh-in systems: Traditional, Virtual, and Satellite.

---

## Traditional On-Site Weigh-Ins

### The Standard Approach

Wrestlers weigh in at the venue, either day-before or day-of.

### Day-Before Weigh-Ins

**Pros:**
- Day-of runs smoother
- More time for corrections
- Less stress for families

**Cons:**
- Staff required night before
- Some wrestlers may not make weight

**Logistics:**
- 1-hour window typical
- Need 2+ scales
- Need volunteers to run
- Record weights in system

### Day-Of Weigh-Ins

**Pros:**
- No extra day commitment
- Can compete at natural weight after cut

**Cons:**
- Creates morning rush
- Limited time for corrections
- Can delay start if problems

**Logistics:**
- Open 60-90 minutes before first session
- Multiple scales needed
- Must close before first match

---

## Virtual Weigh-In

### What Is Virtual Weigh-In?

Wrestlers weigh in at their own facility, verified via video call.

### How It Works

1. Wrestler registers for virtual weigh-in
2. Receives appointment time (Google Meet, Zoom)
3. At scheduled time, joins video call
4. Shows weight on certified scale
5. Staff verifies and records
6. Wrestler receives verification

### Valhalla's Virtual Weigh-In (From Real Experience)

**Requirements:**
- Must weigh at school/team facility
- Must use certified scale (no bathroom scales)
- Must be in singlet
- Must have Google Meet app
- Max 4 wrestlers per appointment

**Fee:** $20 (covers admin costs)

---

## Satellite Weigh-In

### What Is Satellite?

Wrestlers weigh in at another tournament the day before — official weights transferred to your event.

### How It Works

1. Partner with another tournament
2. Wrestlers weigh in at that event
3. That tournament sends official weights
4. You verify and accept
5. No second weigh-in needed

### Best Practices

- Only accept verified official weights
- Get weights in writing (email)
- Have backup if transfer fails

---

## Weigh-In Best Practices

### The Weigh-In Station

| Item | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Certified scale | Required |
| Backup scale | Recommended |
| Table | For processing |
| Volunteers | 2-4 people |
| Forms | Weight sheets |
| Wristbands | For verified wrestlers |

### Processing Flow

1. Wrestler arrives at station
2. Shows ID/confirmation
3. Steps on scale
4. Volunteer records weight
5. Volunteer applies wristband
6. Wrestler clears station

---

## Handling Weight Disputes

### The Protocol

1. **Request for re-weigh:**
   - Allow one re-weigh in singlet only
   - No clothes, no shoes
   - Same scale, same official

2. **Verification:**
   - Check original entry
   - Check scale accuracy
   - Document all weigh-ins

3. **Decision:**
   - Floor manager makes final call
   - If disputed weight confirmed, wrestler placed accordingly
   - Document everything

---

| System | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| **Traditional (day-before)** | Smooth day-of | Extra day |
| **Traditional (day-of)** | No extra trip | Morning rush |
| **Virtual** | No travel | Requires tech |
| **Satellite** | Convenient | Requires partner |

| Extra awards | $3-5/wrestler |
| Food for staff | $100-200 |
| **Total Variable** | ~$10-15/wrestler |

## The Break-Even Formula

```
Break-Even = Fixed Costs ÷ (Entry Fee - Variable Cost per Wrestler)
```

### Example

- Fixed costs: $4,000
- Entry fee: $55
- Variable per wrestler: $12

```
Break-Even = $4,000 ÷ ($55 - $12) = 93 wrestlers
```

**What it means:** You need 93 wrestlers just to break even. Below that, you lose money.
